Technical Notes

01

Daily Pattern Note

Urban Air Case Study

Urban air pollution often changes through the day. Morning and evening traffic can raise particle pollution such as PM2.5, while stronger sunlight in the afternoon can help ozone build through atmospheric chemistry. That means a city may see one kind of pollution peak during busy travel hours and another later in the day as weather, emissions, and sunlight interact.

02

Data Confidence

Limitations and Uncertainty

Data may be modeled or interpolated, and not all cities have direct monitoring stations in the exact location shown here. Values may differ from local EPA monitors because this interface uses gridded Open-Meteo air quality data rather than a single nearby sensor.

03

Method Notes

How This Bulletin Works

The page first uses the Open-Meteo geocoding API to turn a city search into latitude and longitude. It then requests current and hourly air quality data from the Open-Meteo air quality API. Trend messages come from comparing the next three forecast hours with the last three forecast hours in the 24-hour dataset, while interpretation notes use simple AQI and pollutant thresholds to explain what the numbers may mean.

Urban Air Watch monitors city-scale modeled air conditions for public-facing interpretation.
